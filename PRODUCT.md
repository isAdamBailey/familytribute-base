# Product

## Register

brand

## Users

Family members of all ages — both as contributors (authenticated editors adding obituaries, photos, and stories) and as visitors browsing the family's shared history. The public experience is primary: someone discovering the site might be a grandchild, a distant cousin, or a family friend. Older adults are likely represented in the audience; clarity and warmth matter more than novelty.

## Product Purpose

FamilyTribute is a private family archive — a living memorial where families preserve and celebrate the people who made them. It holds obituaries, photos, and stories across generations. Success looks like a family member opening the site and feeling the presence of someone they loved; discovering a story they'd never heard; sharing a picture with a cousin across the country.

This is not a grief platform. It's a celebration of lives lived.

## Brand Personality

Celebratory, story-driven, alive.

Warm and intimate — like being handed a family photo album by someone who loved the people in it. Not solemn or heavy. Not clinical or transactional. The tone is: "look at this remarkable person, look at this life."

## Anti-references

- **Legacy.com and funeral-home listing sites**: Sterile, transactional, ad-supported, form-over-feeling. FamilyTribute should feel nothing like a service you use on the worst day of your life.
- **Grief-heavy / dark memorial aesthetics**: Black backgrounds, heavy silence, design that signals loss. FamilyTribute celebrates, it doesn't mourn.
- **Generic SaaS / startup look**: Indigo-to-violet gradients, eyebrow labels on every section, identical card grids, uppercase tracked CTAs, hero metrics. Should feel human and handmade, not like a product.

## Design Principles

1. **Celebrate life, not mourn loss.** Every design choice — color, typography, spacing, motion — should feel warm and life-affirming. When in doubt, err toward light, warmth, and joy.
2. **Stories and photographs are the heroes.** Design frames content; it never competes with it. The people in the photos, the words in the stories — those are what the user came for.
3. **Personal, not institutional.** Feels handcrafted and intimate, like something a thoughtful family member made. Never feels like a vendor, a platform, or a service.
4. **Timeless over trendy.** Avoid aesthetic choices that will feel dated in five years. Family history is forever; the design should feel like it could last that long too.
5. **Accessible warmth.** WCAG AA as a floor. Older family members are part of the audience — body text should be genuinely readable, not optimally sized for 25-year-olds.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

WCAG AA baseline. Older adults are likely in the audience — prioritize readable body type (minimum 16px, high contrast), clear navigation, and generous tap targets on mobile. Reduced motion support is expected.
